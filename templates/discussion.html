Discussion

Observations

From the visualizations, we were able to make some observations:

  • About 95% (4813) of the victims were male, while only about 5% (255) were female.
  • Black people make up around 27% of the victims, but only are about 12% of the US population (from the 2020 Census). Black people are over-represented in the data set, which supports the idea that there is a bias (conscious or unconscious) in the police force against Black people.
  • White people were under-represented in the data set, making up about 75% of the US population, but only about 51% of the deaths.
  • The age group that was most impacted was people between the ages of 24 and 30 (34%). The age group that was the least affected were people younger than 18 years old (2%).
  • The most common object a victim was recorded as holding was a gun (59%). Unarmed people made up only about 7% of the deaths. This suggests that having a gun while in an encounter with law enforcement increases the probability of being shot and killed.
  • From the map, we observe that most of the fatal encounters occurred in areas of the country that are more populous.

Limitations of the Data Set

With any data, it is important to examine it for any potential biases and confounding factors. One limitation is that this data set is strictly limited to people who were shot by the police and died. This does not include those who were shot and survived, or those who died from other causes.

Additionally, this data set was derived from a Wikipedia page that lists people killed by law enforcement officers within the United States. Because Wikipedia is an open-source website, anyone can add or remove entries to the article. This raises questions about who provides the data, and how many incidents are not recorded in this set. Ultimately, these factors are not within our control, but they could contribute to potential biases in the data.
